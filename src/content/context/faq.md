Q: Who is Luke Duran?
A: Luke Duran is a Technical Product Manager and builder who operates at the intersection of product, engineering, and business. He spends his time building data-driven products professionally while also running and scaling real-world businesses, including Sugar & Spice Book Co. He’s equally comfortable defining product strategy, working through technical systems, and getting hands-on building solutions himself.

Q: What does Luke do professionally?
A: Luke is a Technical Product Manager focused on data-centric and systems-heavy products. His work involves defining requirements for complex systems, collaborating closely with engineering, and ensuring that technical infrastructure translates into meaningful user and business outcomes.

Q: What does Luke do at Pearl?
A: At Pearl, Luke works on data-centric products that require coordination across data pipelines, backend systems, and analytics layers. His role involves defining product requirements for technical systems, ensuring data reliability, and translating complex infrastructure into usable product experiences.

Q: What did Luke work on at The Escape Game?
A: At The Escape Game, Luke worked on customer-facing and operational systems that supported a high-volume, experience-driven business. He focused on improving internal tools, optimizing user flows, and ensuring systems could scale across locations.

Q: How does Luke approach building a product from scratch?
A: Luke starts with the problem, not the solution. He focuses on understanding the user, business constraints, and system constraints before defining anything. From there, he identifies the smallest viable version that delivers value and builds iteratively with tight feedback loops.

Q: How does Luke prioritize features?
A: Luke prioritizes based on impact, effort, and learning. If something delivers meaningful value quickly, reduces uncertainty, or unlocks future capabilities, it moves up. He avoids building anything that doesn’t clearly move one of those levers.

Q: How does Luke handle ambiguous problems?
A: Luke breaks ambiguity down into testable assumptions. Instead of trying to solve everything upfront, he identifies what needs to be true for a solution to work and validates those assumptions as quickly as possible.

Q: How does Luke work with engineers?
A: Luke treats engineers as partners, not ticket-takers. He brings clarity on the “why” and collaborates on the “how.” He’s comfortable getting into technical details when needed, but focuses on enabling engineers to make strong decisions.

Q: How does Luke think about MVP vs long-term vision?
A: Luke holds both in parallel. The MVP should solve a real problem today while pointing toward a scalable future. He avoids shortcuts that create long-term constraints unless there’s a clear reason to take on that tradeoff.

Q: How does Luke define product success?
A: Luke defines success based on real user and business outcomes. That might include engagement, conversion, retention, or operational efficiency depending on the product. He avoids vanity metrics and focuses on signals that indicate value creation.

Q: What is Luke’s experience with data products?
A: Luke has worked on products that rely on structured data, pipelines, and analytics layers. He understands how data flows from ingestion to transformation to consumption and focuses on making that data usable and reliable for end users.

Q: How does Luke work with data and metrics?
A: Luke uses data as a decision-making tool rather than a justification tool. He focuses on identifying the right metrics tied to outcomes and uses those to guide prioritization and iteration.

Q: What is Luke’s experience with AI systems?
A: Luke has hands-on experience building AI-powered tools, including chatbots and automation systems. He focuses on practical applications of AI that improve workflows, reduce manual effort, or enhance user experience.

Q: Why did Luke build an AI chatbot for his portfolio?
A: Luke built the chatbot to demonstrate how he thinks about product, not just talk about it. It’s a practical example of applying retrieval-augmented generation to solve a real problem—helping people quickly understand his experience, thinking, and projects.

Q: What is Sugar & Spice Book Co.?
A: Sugar & Spice Book Co. is a romance-focused bookstore that operates as both a retail business and an event-driven brand. It combines mobile retail, ecommerce, and large-scale events to create a community around romance readers.

Q: What is Luke’s role in Sugar & Spice Book Co.?
A: Luke helps run and scale the business alongside his wife. He focuses on systems, operations, ecommerce, and long-term strategy—building the infrastructure needed to grow the brand.

Q: What has Luke built outside of his full-time job?
A: Luke has built a range of products and systems across ecommerce, AI, and operations. He tends to learn by building and focuses on creating things that work in real-world environments.

Q: What is Luke looking for in his next role?

Q: What type of problems excite Luke most?
A: Luke is most excited by problems at the intersection of systems, data, and user experience—where solving the problem requires both technical depth and strong product thinking.

Q: What does Luke do outside of work?
A: Luke is a dad, a builder, and someone who stays close to the work. He helps run a growing retail and events business and spends a lot of his time building products, systems, and ideas.

Q: What motivates Luke?
A: Luke is motivated by building things that work in the real world. He values ownership, execution, and seeing ideas turn into systems that people actually use.

Q: Tell me about a time Luke built something from scratch.
A: Luke has built multiple systems from scratch, including operational and ecommerce systems for Sugar & Spice Book Co. This required designing workflows, managing inventory constraints, and building scalable processes in a resource-constrained environment.

Q: Tell me about a time Luke handled ambiguity.
A: Luke regularly operates in ambiguous environments, especially when building new products or entering new markets. He approaches this by identifying key unknowns, testing assumptions quickly, and iterating based on what he learns.

Q: Tell me about a time Luke used data to make a decision.
A: Luke uses data to guide decisions across both product and business contexts. In his business, he evaluates sales, customer behavior, and event performance to inform inventory decisions, pricing strategies, and event planning.
