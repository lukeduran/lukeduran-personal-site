# Product Philosophy

I think about product as the intersection of **user value, business leverage, and system reality**. If you ignore any one of those, you either build something people don’t use, something that doesn’t matter to the business, or something that can’t scale.

At a high level, my job is to **reduce uncertainty and increase the odds of building the right thing**.

---

## Start with the Problem, Not the Feature

Most teams jump too quickly to solutions. I try to get extremely clear on:
- What problem are we solving?
- Who actually has this problem?
- Why does it matter right now?

If those aren’t clear, everything downstream gets harder—prioritization, design, engineering, all of it.

---

## Prioritization = Impact, Learning, Leverage

I prioritize work based on three things:

- **Impact** → Does this meaningfully move a user or business metric?
- **Learning** → Does this reduce uncertainty or validate a key assumption?
- **Leverage** → Does this unlock future capabilities or make the system more powerful?

If something doesn’t hit at least one of those, it’s probably not worth doing.

---

## MVPs Should Be Small, But Not Cheap

I believe in shipping fast, but not at the cost of creating long-term constraints.

A good MVP:
- Solves a real problem end-to-end
- Produces signal (not just output)
- Doesn’t box you into a bad architecture

A bad MVP is just a shortcut that creates future rework.

---

## Roadmaps Are Communication Tools, Not Contracts

I don’t think of roadmaps as fixed plans. They’re a way to communicate:
- What we believe matters
- What we’re exploring
- Where we’re headed

If new information comes in and the roadmap doesn’t change, that’s a problem.

---

## Work With Engineers, Not Around Them

The best products come from strong product–engineering partnerships.

My role is to:
- Provide clarity on the problem and constraints
- Bring context from users and the business
- Help prioritize and make tradeoffs

I don’t try to dictate solutions. Engineers should have ownership in *how* things are built.

---

## Data Is a Tool, Not a Crutch

I use data to inform decisions, not justify them after the fact.

Good product decisions combine:
- Quantitative signals (metrics, trends)
- Qualitative insight (user behavior, feedback)
- Judgment (what we believe will work)

If you rely only on data, you move too slowly. If you ignore it, you’re guessing.

---

## Embrace Ambiguity by Breaking It Down

Ambiguity is part of the job. I don’t try to eliminate it upfront—I try to structure it.

I break problems into:
- What do we know?
- What do we assume?
- What do we need to learn?

Then I focus on validating the highest-risk assumptions first.

---

## Great Products Feel Obvious in Hindsight

A great product:
- Solves a real problem
- Is easy to understand and use
- Creates clear value quickly
- Scales without constant friction

Most importantly, it works in the real world—not just in a spec doc.

---

## AI Is a Capability, Not a Strategy

I’m excited about AI, but I don’t think “add AI” is a product strategy.

AI is valuable when it:
- Reduces manual effort
- Improves decision-making
- Enables new user experiences that weren’t previously possible

If it doesn’t clearly improve the outcome, it’s just noise.

---

## Build Things That Actually Work

I bias toward building and shipping over theorizing.

A lot of product thinking looks good in documents but breaks down in reality. I’ve found that the fastest way to learn is to put something in front of users and see what actually happens.

That’s why I spend a lot of time building—whether it’s product features, internal tools, or side projects. It keeps my thinking grounded in what works, not just what sounds good.

---

## Strong Opinion (That I’m Willing to Defend)

Most teams don’t fail because they lack ideas—they fail because they build too much of the wrong thing and don’t learn fast enough.

The best product teams are the ones that:
- Ship quickly
- Learn aggressively
- Adjust constantly

Everything else is secondary.
