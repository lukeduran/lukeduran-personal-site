# Career Narrative

## How I Got Into Product

I'm a product manager who stumbled into technology, but I want to be careful about what that means. I've always been drawn to it — I tried to start an event ticketing technology company in college with no technical experience whatsoever, which probably tells you everything you need to know about how I'm wired. What I didn't have early on was the depth. I taught myself SQL, picked up light coding, and eventually did a full stack development bootcamp because I kept hitting the ceiling of what I could contribute without it. Since then, I've made commits to nearly every codebase I've been responsible for as a PM, and I've been a genuine participant in architectural discussions rather than a bystander to them. At The Escape Game, as the company's first product and technology hire, those decisions were entirely mine to make. I came up through operations, but I grew into a technically fluent one — and I think that combination shapes everything about how I approach my work.

## What I Built at The Escape Game

I started at The Escape Game as a game guide — entry level, front of house — and worked my way up through guest experience specialist and team leader before being invited to join the corporate store operations team in 2017, which was just being established at the time. That store-level progression gave me a ground-level understanding of the business that shaped everything I built later — I knew how the work actually felt before I ever tried to systematize it. On the corporate operations team, I kept running into the ceiling of what good intentions without good data could do, and that's when I started building it myself. I taught myself SQL, stood up reporting infrastructure, and eventually proposed what became TEG Workspace — now called Cosmo — an internal operating system covering game guide software, customer management, liability waivers, scheduling, and store performance metrics. That proposal moved me into a dedicated data and analytics role, where I worked closely with engineering on architecture and built out the company's first real analytics foundations. By 2019, the company was ready to formalize a product function, and I was the one running it. In that role I led Remote Adventures — the virtual escape game platform we launched during COVID when our physical locations went dark — and Unlocked, a digital experience platform that powered branded activations for Amazon Prime Studios, PwC, and the San Francisco 49ers. I spent six years at The Escape Game, working my way from a game guide into the company's first product manager.

## Why I Left The Escape Game

I loved my time at The Escape Game, but over time I started having conversations with product leaders at other companies and noticed a pattern: people had a hard time understanding how an escape room company could have technology products that were genuinely core to operations. It wasn't a knock on the work — the work was real — but I could see that the context wasn't translating, and that if I wanted to build a career in product that opened doors, I needed to get some different experiences on my résumé. That realization is what pushed me to start looking.

## DigitalTenn and Consulting Work

Along the way, I've done consulting work through DigitalTenn — never advertised, never marketed, just made myself available to people in my network who needed help going from idea to execution on the technology side. Online stores, apps for storing healthcare credentials, various operational builds. It's the kind of work I find myself doing naturally because I'm wired that way.

## The Startup Chapter

I spent a short time at a seed-stage social commerce startup that I was genuinely excited about, but we were ultimately building in a space that Instagram and TikTok were actively moving into, which made it difficult to build a durable company out of what was becoming a platform feature. I learned a lot about what early-stage risk really looks like, and what to look for when evaluating a company's position in a market.

## My Time at Pearl

For the last four years, I've been at Pearl, a company at the intersection of AI, home performance, and real estate. It's been a great group of people working on a mission I believe in. I'm proud of what I've built there, but I've reached a point where I'm ready for a bigger stage — more scope, more accountability, harder problems.

## Building Things for Fun

The other thing that's core to who I am: I build things for fun. Not as a side hustle, not with any particular ambition to exit — I just genuinely enjoy the process of starting something. Over the years that's included a coffee delivery business, an event ticketing startup, a merchandise company for artists and musicians, and most recently a romance bookstore and lifestyle brand with my wife. Most of these haven't worked out, and some are still going, but every single one has handed me a problem I'd never faced before. That catalog of experience — operational, strategic, technological — is something I bring into the room every day when I'm solving problems for other people.

## What I'm Looking for Next

What I'm looking for next is a company that's serious about AI, not just in its products but in how it actually operates, and a role where I'm given real accountability for my product areas. I want harder problems and a bigger scope. That's what I'm ready for.
